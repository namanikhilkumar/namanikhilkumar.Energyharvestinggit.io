Abstract

The increasing demand for sustainable and maintenance-free energy sources has led to the development of innovative energy harvesting technologies. This study focuses on designing and optimizing a MEMS-based energy harvesting device using piezoelectric materials to convert beta particle energy from a radioactive isotope into electrical energy. By varying the thickness, length, and width of the piezoelectric materials, the study aims to maximize displacement and voltage output. The device features a copper cantilever structure to efficiently capture electrons emitted by a Nickel-63 source.

Introduction

The project focuses on developing a MEMS-based energy harvesting device that converts beta particle energy into electrical energy via the piezoelectric effect. This device aims to provide a long-term power solution for various applications, such as wireless sensor networks, portable electronics, and cell phones, offering a clean, free, and maintenance-free energy source throughout the device's lifetime.

Problem Statement

Conventional batteries have a limited lifespan and power capacity, necessitating the development of devices that generate power for longer periods. The project addresses this need by designing and modeling a MEMS-based energy harvesting device to convert beta particle energy into electrical energy using the piezoelectric effect.

Objective

The main goal of this project is to maximize the energy harvested by optimizing the shape and material of the piezoelectric transducer. This involves:

  • Modeling a MEMS-based energy harvesting device to convert beta particle energy from a radioactive isotope to electrical energy via the piezoelectric effect.
  • Varying the thickness, length, and width of piezoelectric materials to achieve maximum displacement and voltage.
  • Constructing a cantilever made of copper with dimensions of 500µm × 100µm × 2µm, chosen for its effectiveness in capturing most electrons.

Literature Survey

Energy harvesting technologies have gained significant attention over the past two decades as alternatives to conventional power sources for small-sized and low-power electronic devices. The need for periodic recharging or replacement of batteries has been a consistent issue for portable, remote, and implantable devices.

Piezoelectric Energy Harvesting

Definition and Mechanism: Piezoelectric energy harvesting converts dynamic mechanical energy into electrical energy using piezoelectric materials. This method focuses on harvesting low-level energy (microwatts to milliwatts) to power low-power electronics.

Advantages: Piezoelectric transduction is advantageous over thermal and solar harvesting because ambient vibrations are persistent due to operational conditions, making it reliable and useful in embedded systems where thermal and solar energy might be absent.

Applications: Common applications include providing energy for low-power electronics such as embedded electronics, implantable biomedical devices, wireless sensor nodes, and portable electronics. These systems offer a permanent, autonomous power source, reducing costs associated with battery replacement and enabling deployment in remote or embedded locations.

Methodology

The device is designed to convert beta particle energy into electrical energy through the piezoelectric effect. The cantilever's dimensions were optimized to enhance energy conversion efficiency. A copper cantilever, with dimensions 500 µm × 100 µm × 2 µm, was selected to effectively capture electrons from the Nickel-63 source.

Materials Used:

  • Piezoelectric Material: PZT-5H (Lead Zirconate Titanate), known for its high piezoelectric constant and energy conversion efficiency.
  • Substrate Material: Silicon, selected for its mechanical properties and compatibility with MEMS fabrication.
  • Radioactive Isotope: Nickel (Ni), suitable for beta particle emission.
  • Cantilever Material: Copper, dimensions 500 µm × 100 µm × 2 µm, chosen to capture electrons effectively.
  • Electrodes: Gold, for excellent conductivity and inertness, ensuring efficient charge collection.

Parameters: Varying the thickness, length, and width of the piezoelectric materials to achieve maximum displacement and voltage.

Results and Analysis

Initial simulations reveal that the dimensions of the piezoelectric materials significantly impact energy harvesting efficiency. The optimized device structure shows a substantial increase in voltage output and displacement, indicating its potential to power low-power electronic devices. Key findings include:

  • Displacement Magnitude (µm): The optimal dimensions of the piezoelectric cantilever were identified to maximize displacement.
  • Electric Potential: Analysis of the relationship between displacement and electrical potential demonstrated enhanced energy conversion.
